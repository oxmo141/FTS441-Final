1. Introduction


Formula SAE (FSAE) is an intercollegiate engineering design competition organised by the Society of Automotive Engineers International, in which university teams design, manufacture, and race formula-style vehicles. Each car is evaluated on its design, cost, efficiency, and performance across a series of dynamic and static events, as well as its compliance with safety regulations during technical inspection. Competitors are additionally assessed on their team management, engineering judgement, technical knowledge, and design process.

1.1. Team Composition


The R26e team consists of 27 members drawn from a range of engineering disciplines. The team is primarily divided into two core technical principals — Powertrain and Chassis — each of which is further subdivided into specialist departments responsible for specific areas of the vehicle's design and development.

1.2. R25e Post Competition Remarks


R25e achieved its highest-ever overall standing of 7th place at the 2025 Formula SAE Electric competition in Michigan, USA. The team secured a top-10 finish in the majority of events, demonstrating a well-rounded level of performance. Nevertheless, several areas for improvement were identified, particularly in the dynamic events, where the team encountered challenges related to handling consistency and system reliability. A key issue was the battery management system, which exhibited thermal overheating during testing and competition runs, compromising both performance and safety. In addition, the vehicle's handling characteristics were suboptimal, with recurring oversteer tendencies observed throughout the season.

1.3. Team Goal & Problem Statement Analysis

The overarching team goal for the R26e season is to reduce the average lap time in the Endurance event by 10% through improved system reliability. In support of this goal, the Vehicle Dynamics Suspension department has established a specific objective to enhance the car's handling characteristics. To better understand the root causes of the observed handling issues, a comprehensive vehicle dynamics analysis was conducted using R25e competition and testing data.

1.3.1. Skidpad Competition Performance


The Skidpad event measures a vehicle's maximum steady-state cornering capability by recording the total time taken to complete one left-hand and one right-hand circle. The event is specifically designed to evaluate suspension characteristics and tune-ability for maximum lateral grip while minimising the influence of driver inputs during transitional manoeuvres (Goslawski, Snyder, 2023).


Skidpad performance can be assessed using a G-G diagram, which plots lateral acceleration against longitudinal acceleration throughout the run. Since the skidpad tests steady-state cornering, an ideal run would exhibit minimal variance in lateral acceleration. However, as shown in Figure 5, the vehicle experienced significant fluctuations in lateral acceleration during both the left-hand and right-hand circles. This inconsistency indicates that the vehicle was unable to sustain a constant cornering force, resulting in lost lap time potential and confirming the presence of handling issues.

1.4. The Problem Statement

The skidpad performance data reveals significant fluctuations in lateral acceleration during cornering, suggesting an underlying handling instability. This report investigates lateral load transfer characteristics and suspension dynamics to identify the root cause of these issues and propose targeted design modifications for R26e.



2. Suspension Design Optimisation

The dynamic behaviour of a vehicle can be characterised by two primary performance attributes: Roadholding and Handling. Roadholding refers to the vehicle's ability to maintain consistent tyre contact with the road surface by managing vertical motion along the z-axis (heave) and rotational motion about the y-axis (pitch) (Pütz, Serné, 2025). Handling, on the other hand, describes the lateral dynamics of the vehicle — specifically, its ability to negotiate corners quickly and with stability (Pütz, Serné, 2025).


2.1. Suspension Stiffness

R25e was configured with a high suspension spring rate of 300 lb/in at both axles. While high spring rates are commonly employed to reduce body roll and improve cornering responsiveness, they do so at the expense of roadholding compliance. Under typical FSAE Michigan competition conditions — where the track surface is smooth and well-maintained — stiffer suspension setups are generally acceptable. However, the analysis presented in this section suggests that the high spring rates used on R25e may have contributed to the handling inconsistencies observed during dynamic events. To investigate this further, the following sections examine the vehicle's roll dynamics and damper travel behaviour.


2.1.1. What Happens During Roll?


During cornering, the lateral centrifugal force acting at the vehicle's centre of gravity (CG) induces a rolling moment about the roll centre. This roll motion causes the outer suspension to compress (bump travel) and the inner suspension to extend (droop travel). If the droop travel limit is reached before sufficient cornering force is generated, the inner wheel lifts off the ground, resulting in a complete loss of traction and reduced lateral stability at that axle.

2.1.2. Droop and Bump Travel Analysis


Static spring compression calculations revealed that the 300 lb/in springs compress by only 10.223 mm under static loading conditions. This leaves 10.223 mm of available droop travel and 46.777 mm of remaining bump travel, against a total shock stroke of 57 mm — the maximum travel of the Ohlins TTX25 MkII dampers used on both R25e and R26e.


Figure 9 shows the rear damper travel recorded during the competition skidpad event. In this plot, positive damper travel values correspond to droop (extension), while negative values correspond to bump (compression). The data indicates a maximum bump travel of approximately 8 mm and a maximum droop travel of approximately 6 mm. As the car was not driven at the limit during this event, no critical anomalies in damper behaviour are apparent. For a more informative assessment, the autocross data — where the vehicle was driven more aggressively — is examined below.


The autocross damper travel data in Figure 10 reveals a consistent ceiling in droop travel at approximately 6 mm for both the left and right rear dampers, while bump travel reaches a maximum of approximately 12 mm. The hard plateau in droop travel confirms that the dampers are reaching their full extension limit during cornering. This is a clear indicator that the suspension spring rates are too high, restricting the available droop travel and causing the inner rear wheel to lift off the ground once the droop limit is reached.

2.2. Final Conclusion - Suspension Stiffness

The trade-offs associated with modifying suspension spring rates are significant and must be carefully considered. The analysis presented above provides clear evidence that the inner tyre is lifting off the road surface during cornering, a direct consequence of insufficient droop travel caused by the high spring rates.


As illustrated in Figure 11, dynamic load transfer between the inner and outer tyres reduces the overall lateral force capacity of the axle due to the non-linear relationship between vertical tyre load and lateral grip. When a 4 kN load differential is present, the combined lateral force of the axle falls below the theoretical value achievable under equal loading. For a racing application, it is therefore desirable to minimise dynamic load variation across each axle (Frömming, 2023). When the inner wheel fully lifts off the ground, the entire axle load is transferred to the outer tyre, causing 100% lateral load transfer — the worst-case scenario for cornering performance.

In rally motorsport, deliberately lifting the inner rear wheel during hard cornering is an established technique. For front-wheel drive vehicles, this is used to increase the yaw rate by overloading the outer rear tyre and rotating the car into the corner. However, for a rear-wheel drive platform such as R25e and R26e, the consequences of inner rear wheel lift on traction and stability are less predictable and warrant further investigation.

For R26e, the suspension spring rates have been revised to 225 lb/in at the front axle and 200 lb/in at the rear axle. This reduction is intended to increase the available droop travel, thereby reducing the likelihood of inner wheel lift during dynamic events. The front axle retains a higher spring rate than the rear to preserve responsive turn-in behaviour and to manage oversteer during bump inputs. The softer rear spring rate is intended to increase mechanical grip and reduce contact patch variation — particularly important for the driven wheels. The principal trade-off of this change is an increase in body roll and a reduction in overall cornering stiffness, both of which will need to be carefully managed through suspension geometry and damper tuning.

Comprehensive testing and validation will be required to confirm whether these modifications successfully reduce the lateral acceleration fluctuations observed during the R25e skidpad event and result in a measurable improvement in overall vehicle handling.

3. Cornering Dynamics Modelling

Understanding how loads are distributed across individual wheels is fundamental to analysing and optimising vehicle performance. Wheel loads directly govern a car's maximum steady-state cornering capability and are therefore a critical consideration in both the design and setup phases. While wheel loads are inherently dynamic and continuously changing in response to road conditions, driver inputs, and suspension behaviour, quasi-static assumptions provide a tractable framework for calculating these loads in a structured and interpretable manner — offering valuable insight into their magnitude, distribution, and sensitivity to design parameters (Milliken, Milliken, 1995).

3.1. Equation of Motion

The equation of motion governing the roll dynamics of the vehicle in steady-state cornering is expressed as: \( I_x \ddot{\phi} + C_\phi \dot{\phi} + K_\phi \phi = M_{roll} \), where \( I_x \) is the mass moment of inertia of the sprung mass about the longitudinal (roll) axis, \( C_\phi \) is the equivalent roll damping coefficient, \( K_\phi \) is the total roll stiffness, \( \phi \) is the vehicle roll angle, and \( M_{roll} \) is the roll moment induced by lateral acceleration during cornering.


The mass moment of inertia \( I_x \) is extracted from the vehicle's CAD model in SolidWorks, which provides an estimated representation of the geometry and mass distribution of the sprung components. It is important to exclude the unsprung mass from this calculation, as the unsprung components do not participate in the roll dynamics of the sprung body. Including the unsprung mass would result in an overestimation of the roll inertia and introduce error into the dynamic model.


The roll stiffness \( K_\phi \) and roll damping coefficient \( C_\phi \) are derived from the suspension geometry together with the spring, damper, and anti-roll bar (ARB) rates. Assuming a motion ratio of 1, the spring roll stiffness is given by \( K_{\phi,\text{spring}} = \frac{K_{\text{spring}} \cdot t^2}{2} \), and the roll damping coefficient is given by \( C_{\phi} = \frac{C_{\text{damper}} \cdot t^2}{2} \), where \( t \) is the track width of the vehicle. Since the ARB acts in parallel with the spring roll stiffness, the total roll stiffness is expressed as \( K_{\phi,\text{total}} = K_{\phi,\text{spring}} + K_{\phi,\text{ARB}} \).

3.1.1. Steady-State Analysis

During cornering, the centrifugal force acting at the vehicle's centre of gravity generates a rolling moment about the roll centre. This moment is resisted by the suspension at both axles, distributing the roll angle across the chassis in proportion to each axle's roll stiffness and the torsional rigidity of the chassis structure. The steady-state roll angle distribution is determined by assembling and solving a stiffness and load matrix in MATLAB:

\[ \text{Stiffness Matrix } (A) = \begin{bmatrix} k_{\text{roll},f} & 0 & -TR \\[6pt] 0 & k_{\text{roll},r} & TR \\[6pt] 1 & -1 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \]
\[ \text{Load Matrix } (M) = \begin{bmatrix} M_{fu} + \dfrac{b}{a+b}\,M_s \\[10pt] M_{ru} + \dfrac{a}{a+b}\,M_s \\[10pt] 0 \end{bmatrix} \]
\[ \text{Roll Angle Solution } (R) = A^{-1} M = \begin{bmatrix} \phi_f \\[10pt] \phi_r \\[10pt] \phi_{\text{body}} \end{bmatrix} \]

3.1.2. Transient Numerical Analysis

Following the steady-state roll angle calculation, the transient lateral load transfer at the front and rear axles is computed by time-stepping the roll dynamics using the Dormand-Prince Runge-Kutta (RK45) integration scheme. The state-space formulation of the two-axle roll system is expressed as:

\[ \begin{bmatrix} \dot{x}_1 \\ \dot{x}_2 \\ \dot{x}_3 \\ \dot{x}_4 \end{bmatrix} = \underbrace{ \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 & 0 \\[4pt] -\dfrac{k_f}{I} & -\dfrac{c_f}{I} & 0 & 0 \\[10pt] 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 \\[4pt] 0 & 0 & -\dfrac{k_r}{I} & -\dfrac{c_r}{I} \end{bmatrix} }_{A} \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \\ x_3 \\ x_4 \end{bmatrix} + \underbrace{ \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\[4pt] \dfrac{M_f}{I} \\[10pt] 0 \\[4pt] \dfrac{M_r}{I} \end{bmatrix} }_{\mathbf{b}} \]
\[ \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \\ x_3 \\ x_4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \text{Front Roll Angle} \quad (\theta_f) \\[6pt] \text{Front Roll Angular Velocity} \quad (\omega_f) \\[6pt] \text{Rear Roll Angle} \quad (\theta_r) \\[6pt] \text{Rear Roll Angular Velocity} \quad (\omega_r) \end{bmatrix} \]




Figures 14 and 15 show the time histories of the roll angle and roll angular velocity obtained from the numerical integration. These transient quantities govern the elastic component of lateral load transfer at each axle, which is also plotted in Figures 16 and 17. As expected, the geometric load transfer — which depends solely on the vehicle's mass, roll center heights, and lateral acceleration — remains constant for a given steady-state condition and is independent of the suspension setup. In contrast, the elastic load transfer evolves dynamically during body roll and only reaches its steady-state value once the chassis settles at the equilibrium roll angle. This transient behaviour is directly influenced by the spring and damper rates at each axle.

4. Suspension Setup

Using the lateral load transfer analysis from the numerical model, the suspension setup can be systematically optimised to achieve a more balanced load transfer distribution between the front and rear axles. This section discusses the selection of spring and anti-roll bar rates to achieve the target load transfer distribution and their effect on handling balance. The selection of appropriate damping rates to complement the spring setup and produce a stable, predictable handling response is also addressed.

4.1. Damping Roll Optimisation

Damping optimisation for roll control focuses on tuning the damping characteristics of the suspension to govern how quickly and smoothly the vehicle body rolls into a corner. The objective is to achieve a suitably rapid roll response without introducing excessive overshoot or a significant delay in rise time. Correctly tuned damping is essential for maintaining consistent tyre contact with the road, ensuring chassis stability, and producing a predictable handling response.


As illustrated in Figure 20, under low damping conditions the roll angle oscillates significantly before settling to a steady-state value. This underdamped response produces an unstable and inconsistent handling feel, as the tyre contact loads vary continuously during the transient phase. Prolonged oscillation can also cause periodic tyre unloading, reducing peak lateral grip and increasing lap time variability.


The Nelder-Mead simplex algorithm was used to identify the optimal damping coefficient for a range of target overshoot values. The rise time — defined as the time taken for the roll angle to reach 90% of its steady-state value — was monitored alongside overshoot to ensure that reducing overshoot did not introduce an unacceptably slow suspension response. As shown in Figure 21, the zero-overshoot case results in a noticeably delayed rise time, indicating an overdamped condition in which the suspension responds sluggishly to changes in lateral load.


The sensitivity plot in Figure 22 confirms that as target overshoot increases, the corresponding rise time decreases monotonically. Notably, the rise time penalty for reducing overshoot from 10% to 0% is approximately 0.04 seconds — a significant delay relative to the 0.01 seconds change observed across the 10%–20% overshoot range. This disproportionate sensitivity reinforces the selection of 10%–20% overshoot as the operating target, balancing roll control performance with transient response speed.


At a target overshoot of 10%–20%, the optimal roll damping coefficients are in the range of 3000–4000 Nms/rad for both the front and rear axles, as shown in Figure 23. This range provides an effective balance between suppressing excessive body roll and preserving a responsive suspension reaction during cornering.

4.2. Lateral Load Transfer Distribution

Lateral load transfer distribution describes how the total lateral load transfer is apportioned between the front and rear axles during cornering. It is one of the primary tuning parameters governing a vehicle's handling balance: a distribution biased toward the rear axle promotes oversteer, while a front-biased distribution promotes understeer. Achieving a well-balanced distribution is therefore critical for producing a stable, controllable, and consistent handling response.


Figure 24 shows the lateral load transfer distribution for the R25e spring configuration (300 lb/in at both axles). The distribution is clearly biased toward the rear axle, indicating that the rear suspension carries a disproportionately large share of the total lateral load transfer.


Figure 25 shows the lateral load transfer distribution for the revised R26e spring configuration (225 lb/in front, 200 lb/in rear). Compared to Figure 24, the distribution is significantly more balanced between the front and rear axles, indicating that both axles now share a more proportionate fraction of the total lateral load transfer. This more neutral distribution is expected to reduce the tendency for rear-axle grip saturation, producing a more predictable and balanced handling response during cornering.

4.3. Ride Analysis


Ride analysis evaluates the vehicle's vertical dynamic response to road inputs, characterising how effectively the suspension isolates the chassis from road irregularities while maintaining consistent tyre loading. The analysis models the vehicle as a two DOF system incorporating heave and pitch motions, and the results are used to assess roadholding capability and the adequacy of the chosen spring and damper rates.


Bounce and pitch are the two fundamental modes of vertical motion in a vehicle's suspension system. Bounce describes a pure vertical oscillation of the entire body, while pitch describes a rotational oscillation about the vehicle's lateral axis — causing the front and rear to move in opposition. Both modes are governed by the suspension spring and damper rates, and their natural frequencies and damping ratios must be carefully tuned to achieve an acceptable balance between roadholding and handling.

The MATLAB simulation results presented in Figures 28 and 29 represent the R25e and R26e suspension setups respectively. Both setups exhibit overdamped pitch and bounce modes, confirming that neither configuration is prone to sustained oscillation in response to road inputs. The softer R26e setup yields marginally lower peak front and rear axle displacements, a slightly reduced peak pitch angle, and a lower peak CG heave value. These results indicate that the revised spring rates produce a modestly improved vertical dynamic response, with the softer springs enabling the suspension to absorb road inputs more effectively. Consequently, the R26e configuration is expected to deliver improved roadholding and handling consistency during steady-state dynamic events.

However, for transient dynamic events, R26e's softer setup has a lower pitch and bounce natural frequency, which may result in a slower response to rapid changes in road conditions and driver inputs. This trade-off will need to be carefully evaluated through testing and driver feedback to ensure that the handling benefits of the softer setup are not offset by a reduction in responsiveness during transient events.

4.4. R26e Test-Run Data and Validation

To validate the theoretical analysis and confirm that the revised suspension setup achieves the intended improvements in handling performance, test runs were conducted with R26e using both the original 300 lb/in spring configuration and the revised 225 lb/in front, 200 lb/in rear configuration. Skidpad test runs were selected for this validation, as they provide a controlled environment to evaluate cornering performance and handling consistency under steady-state lateral acceleration conditions.


Figure 30 shows the linear potentiometer displacement plotted against lateral acceleration during an R26e test run using the original R25e 300 lb/in spring configuration. The data reveals that both the left and right rear potentiometers consistently read a value of 0 mm (fully extended) at lateral accelerations beyond approximately 0.5 g. This saturated extension indicates that the dampers have reached their full droop travel limit and are no longer able to maintain tyre contact with the road surface. As a result, the inner rear wheel lifts off the ground, causing the car to lose traction — a condition that is highly detrimental to cornering performance.


Figure 31 presents the equivalent data for the revised R26e spring configuration (225 lb/in front, 200 lb/in rear). With the softer spring rates, the potentiometer displacement only approaches 0 mm at lateral accelerations exceeding 1.0 g at the front, and approximately 1.4 g at the rear — at which point approximately 1 mm of additional droop travel remains. This confirms that the revised setup substantially increases the effective droop travel envelope, allowing the suspension to maintain tyre contact across a wider range of cornering loads.



Figures 32 and 33 show the G-G diagrams for R26e with the 300 lb/in and the 225 lb/in front, 200 lb/in rear spring configurations respectively. The G-G diagram plots longitudinal acceleration against lateral acceleration, providing a visual representation of the vehicle's dynamic performance envelope. The data confirms that the revised spring setup allows R26e to achieve more consistent lateral grip, as the scatter plot shows a dense clustering of data points within 0.5 g to 1.6 g of lateral acceleration. In contrast, the 300 lb/in configuration shows a much wider scatter, particularly at higher lateral accelerations, indicating more frequent and severe tyre slip due to the inner rear wheel lifting off the ground. The improved consistency in Figure 33 is a strong indicator of enhanced handling performance and better utilisation of available grip during cornering.

5. Steering System Assembly


The R26e steering system comprises four principal sub-assemblies: the steering wheel, steering shaft, steering rod, and steering rack, each discussed in the following sections.

5.1. Steering Rack Assembly


The steering rack converts rotational input from the steering wheel into linear motion to actuate the front wheels. The inboard steering clevis is secured to the rack with Loctite thread-locking compound to prevent loosening under cyclic loading.

5.2. Steering Shaft Assembly


The steering shaft transmits rotational input from the steering wheel to the rack. A double universal joint (U-joint) accommodates angular misalignment between the two sub-systems, and a steering brace constrains the column against deflection under load.

5.2.1. Steering Brace


A dedicated welding jig ensures the TIG-welded steering brace conforms to the CAD geometry. Sacrificial spacers are installed before welding and removed afterwards to preserve internal clearances and minimise distortion from weld shrinkage.


The brace must be fully seam welded and allowed to cool completely while still fixtured; premature jig removal releases residual thermal stresses and causes warping.


On R26e, the brace was removed before fully cooling, causing it to deviate from the intended geometry. Corrective re-welding of a supporting member restored alignment, but introduced additional heat-affected zones that may reduce local weld strength and create stress concentrations in the structure.

5.2.2. Steering Column


The steering column uses a press-fitted support bearing for smooth shaft rotation. During assembly, the column tube outer diameter was found to exceed the bearing bore tolerance, requiring hand-sanding to achieve the correct fit. This may introduce marginal play in the shaft, reducing steering feel precision under dynamic loading.


The double U-joint is spot welded first to maintain concentric yoke alignment, followed by a full seam weld applied with intermittent cooling pauses to prevent heat damage to the internal bearing components. On R26e, overheating caused the internal lubricant to vaporise and escape. The dry joint remains functional for oscillatory steering loads, but accelerated needle bearing wear is expected over time, which should be addressed before competition.

5.3. Installation



Following chassis installation, steering stops are fitted to the rack to define travel limits. The steering wheel is levelled using a digital inclinometer, and the final fixing hole in the column is drilled to lock the shaft at the neutral position.

5.4. Cost Report


The total cost of the steering system assembly is approximately $500, with the steering rack and shaft being the most significant contributors due to their precision manufacturing requirements. The custom steering brace and associated welding costs are also notable, particularly given the need for rework to correct geometric distortion. Future iterations could explore alternative brace designs or fabrication methods to reduce costs while maintaining structural integrity.
